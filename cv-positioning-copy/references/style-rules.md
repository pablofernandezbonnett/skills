# Style Rules

## Base Principle

- Credibility is more important than maximum optimization.
- The candidate profile is the source of truth.
- If a sentence cannot be defended with a real example in a short interview answer, weaken or rewrite it.

## Truth Boundaries

- Do not present exposure as ownership.
- Do not present collaboration with cloud, infrastructure, or DevOps teams as cloud or DevOps strength.
- Do not present technical influence as formal leadership unless the role really had that scope.
- Make honest gaps explicit in the source-of-truth material so they do not leak into CVs or cover letters as false strengths.

## Natural Wording

- Sound human, not AI-optimized.
- Prefer wording the candidate could realistically say out loud.
- Avoid phrases that sound reusable for any senior engineer.
- Avoid corporate filler and inflated tone.

## No Buzzword Rule

- Do not use terms like `scalable`, `reliable`, `maintainable`, `production-ready`, `platform`, or `architecture` without anchoring them to a real system or problem.
- Replace abstract quality claims with concrete evidence:
  - system type
  - customer-facing flow
  - integration type
  - performance problem
  - stakeholder context
  - delivery or maintenance responsibility

## Punch Without Overselling

- Punch comes from specificity, not inflation.
- Prefer concrete problem framing over self-evaluation.
- Show what kinds of systems and problems the candidate has already handled.
- Make the strongest areas visible early, especially if they match the target role.

## Narrative Consistency

- CV, LinkedIn, and cover letter should be variations of the same truth.
- The master CV holds the stable identity.
- Offer-specific variants should adjust emphasis, not rewrite the candidate.
- Each experience block should add a new signal. If two blocks say the same thing, rewrite one.

## Platform vs Project Precision

- Distinguish technology/platform from project/product.
- Keep naming precise.
- Example: `SAP Commerce / Hybris` is the platform or environment. `SAP Store` is the concrete product/project.

## Summary and About Rules

- Lead with identity, then strongest domain, then evidence.
- Do not stack too many dense terms into one sentence if readability drops.
- Avoid repeated phrasing like `my strongest` across adjacent sentences.
- Keep enough detail to be concrete, but not so much that the summary feels overloaded.

## Experience Rules

- Each role should make clear:
  - what type of system it was
  - what the candidate actually did
  - what kind of problem area they touched
- Mention technologies selectively when they improve transferability or credibility.
- Keep strong roles textured; compress older or less relevant roles first.

## Repetition Control

- Watch for repeated wording families such as:
  - production
  - maintainability
  - stability
  - evolution
  - worked
  - strongest
- Fix repetition by changing the information angle, not only the synonym.

## Skills Rules

- Skills should contain real stack and domain keywords, not narrative claims.
- Do not add ATS keywords that the candidate cannot defend.
- Projects do not automatically belong in the skills section if they fit better in experience.

## Japan Market Fit

- Help the reader answer quickly:
  - Is the candidate in Japan?
  - Do they need sponsorship?
  - What kind of engineer are they?
  - Is the experience real and usable?
  - Where can this person fit?
- Prefer sober, clear, stable positioning over hype.
- The candidate should feel interviewable and placeable, not inflated.

## Compression Rules

- Cut redundancy before cutting signal.
- Compress older experience before weakening strong recent roles.
- Keep visible if relevant:
  - current location / sponsorship status
  - core identity
  - strongest domain
  - strongest product or commerce evidence
  - integrations / checkout / payment-related work when real

## Final Check

Before finishing, check:
- Does the wording still sound like the same person across documents?
- Does anything sound stronger than the candidate profile supports?
- Has any key distinction been blurred, such as platform vs project or exposure vs ownership?
- Is the document trying to impress more than it is trying to be defensible?
